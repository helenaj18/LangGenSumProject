On May 8, 2018, the SEC filed a complaint in the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York against a former CEO of a California-based energy company.  The SEC's complaint alleges that Michael Burke, the former CEO of AECOM, misappropriated approximately $1.2 billion in investor funds to support his personal use of the funds.  According to the complaint, Burke misappropriated more than $1.2 billion in investor funds to fund his personal use of the funds.  The SEC's complaint, filed in the U.S. District Court, charges Burke with violating the antifraud provisions of Section 17(a) of the Securities Act of 1933 and Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and Rule 10b-5 thereunder.  Without admitting or denying the allegations in the complaint, Burke has consented to the entry of a final judgment that permanently enjoins him from violating the antifraud provisions of Sections 17(a)(1) and (3) of the Securities Act and Section 10(b) of the Exchange Act and Rule 10b-5 thereunder, and orders him to pay disgorgement and prejudgment interest thereon.  The settlement is subject to court approval.  The SEC's complaint charges Burke with violating Section 17(a) of the Securities Act, Section 10(b) of the Exchange Act, Rule 10b-5 thereunder, and Sections 206(1) and 206(2) of the Investment Advisers Act of 1940.  Without admitting or denying the SEC's allegations, Burke has agreed to the entry of a final judgment permanently enjoining him from future violations of the antifraud provisions of Sections 206(1), 206(2), and 206(3) of the Investment Advisers Act of 1940 and Rule 206(4)-8 thereunder.  The settlement is subject to court approval by the court.  The SEC's investigation, which is continuing, is being conducted by Andrew Kaplowitz and John Wittmann of the SEC's Boston Regional Office.  The litigation will be led by Mr. Wotring.  The SEC appreciates the assistance of the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Southern District of New York, the Federal Bureau of Investigation, and the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority.
